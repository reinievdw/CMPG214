The hardest SU2 principle to apply was separating the inform function from the persuade function. I always thought they were the same thing, but this activity showed me they're not. When you're writing a public statement during a crisis, every fact you share is also trying to win their trust back — so keeping them separate is harder than it sounds.
What failed in the original crisis communication was the channel choice. The CIO sent a highly technical email to a CEO with a finance background. According to Daft and Lengel's channel richness theory, a crisis this serious needed a rich channel like a phone call, not a lean one like email. The message arrived but the meaning didn't — that's a Shannon-Weaver Level B semantic failure.
If this were real, I would make sure the Head of Communications is notified at the same time as the CEO. Waiting for internal clarity before informing the public cost RetailCo hours it didn't have.
